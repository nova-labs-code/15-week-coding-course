Week 16: User Experience & Design – Extended

This week focuses on **creating a smooth, user-friendly website experience**, with added exercises and tips to help refine your design and usability thinking.

Day 1: Navigation Design

Learn: Menus guide users. Use clear labels, logical ordering, and visible navigation. Avoid long menus and confusing labels.

Do: Sketch your main menu including top-level pages and possible submenus. Consider drop-downs, hamburger menus, or sticky headers.

Mini Exercise: Draw two navigation versions and pick the one that allows users to reach any page in 2–3 clicks.
Tip: Use common words like "Home", "About", "Contact" to avoid confusing visitors.
Day 2: Mobile-First Thinking

Learn: Most users browse on mobile. Start with small screens, then scale up. Focus on readability, touch-friendly buttons, and vertical layouts.

Do: Draw a mobile layout of your homepage. Include header, content sections, buttons, and footer. Ensure text and buttons are easy to see and tap.

Mini Exercise: Check your design with small sticky notes or screenshots to simulate a phone screen.
Tip: Mobile-first design avoids clutter and forces prioritization of content.
Day 3: Accessibility

Learn: Make your site usable for all users. Consider:

  • Color contrast for readability
  • Alt text for images
  • Keyboard navigation
  • Readable fonts and sizes

Do: Test your page using high-contrast mode, keyboard navigation, and screen reader tools.

Mini Exercise: List 3 accessibility issues you notice on a sample website and suggest fixes.
Tip: Accessibility improves SEO and helps more users interact with your content.
Day 4: Visual Hierarchy

Learn: Guide attention with headings, spacing, images, and highlights. Important info should be most prominent.

Do: Organize a sample page with headings, paragraphs, and images. Make main points stand out and less important info smaller or secondary.

Mini Exercise: Create a page mockup showing 3 levels of headings, text blocks, and one highlighted call-to-action.
Tip: White space is powerful. Avoid clutter to let users focus on key content.
Day 5: User Flow Testing

Learn: Simulate users completing tasks like signing up or finding info. Look for confusing steps.

Do: Ask a friend to navigate your mockup and note friction points. Focus on tasks like submitting a form or accessing content.

Mini Exercise: Document at least 2 usability issues and propose solutions (e.g., clearer buttons, simpler form).
Tip: Observing others is one of the fastest ways to spot UX problems.
Day 6: Feedback Systems

Learn: Collect user feedback with forms, polls, or surveys. Feedback helps refine design and content.

Do: Create a simple feedback form with 2–3 questions about content and layout. Make it easy to submit and read responses.

Mini Exercise: Test the form with a friend and note if they find it clear and easy to use.
Tip: Even short surveys can reveal hidden usability issues.
Day 7: Weekly Review

Learn: Combine lessons from navigation, mobile design, accessibility, visual hierarchy, user flow, and feedback.

Do: Update your mockup or prototype with improvements. Test navigation paths, headings, mobile layout, and feedback forms.

Mini Exercise: Create a checklist covering:
  • All main pages accessible
  • Mobile readability
  • Accessible colors and fonts
  • Clear visual hierarchy
  • Functional feedback system
Tip: A refined prototype will save time and frustration once you start coding.
